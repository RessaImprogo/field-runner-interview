I
Introduction & Icebreaker
Set tone. Did they actually read the post?
Before you start
Keep it warm and relaxed — you want their real personality, not a rehearsed performance. Watch how they carry themselves before you even ask anything. First impressions here are data.
1 Your intro & opening question
"Hey ___, thanks for coming in! I'm ___, one of the recruiters here at Proliance — we're a roofing and general contracting company based in the US. This role is really the engine that keeps our production and sales teams moving in the field. Before I get into anything formal — what made you apply for this one specifically?"
Why this question works
You're not asking what they know — you're asking what pulled them toward it. Someone who read it and connected to something specific is very different from someone bulk-applying who barely remembers the post.
✓ Strong answer
Mentions something specific — outdoor work, driving, variety, the pay
Sounds like something clicked when they read it
Talks about what they want, not just what they need
✗ Watch for
"I just needed a job" with nothing behind it
Can't recall what the post actually said
Generic — "I like people" with no substance
Notes
Applied with intention
II
Physical & Safety
Can they actually do the physical work?
Context for you
Heights is the first disqualifier. Training handles the drone and inspection process — it does not handle fear of heights. If someone is genuinely scared, they are a liability and a retention risk. Make it safe to be honest so the yes you get back actually means something.
1 Heights must-have
"___, part of this role is getting on rooftops — including steep ones. I want to be upfront about that. Walk me through your relationship with heights — be real with me."
✓ Strong answer
Has actual experience — construction, cleaning, roofing, military
Confident, no visible hesitation
Asks about safety protocols — engaged, not reckless
✗ Red flags
"I think I can handle it" — uncertain even after the jolt
Body language shifts — arms cross, looks down
Asks if it can be avoided or if someone else can go up
If they hesitate — follow-up questions
"What's the highest you've been on a ladder or roof before?"
"If you got up there and it felt worse than expected — what would you do?"
Exit speech — if they don't pass heights
"___, I really appreciate your honesty — and I mean that. This part of the role is something we can't work around, and I'd rather be upfront with you than put you in a situation that's not the right fit. You've got strong qualities and I think there's a role out there that's a better match. I'll keep your info on file and if something comes up that doesn't involve rooftop work, we'll reach out. Thanks so much for your time today."
Notes
Comfortable with heights
2 Physical stamina must-have
"Describe the most physically demanding workday you've had, ___. What was it, and how did you feel by the end of it?"
Why ask it this way
You're not asking "are you fit" — you're asking for evidence. A story reveals what their actual baseline is. Someone whose hardest day was a long office shift is not the same as someone who's done construction, delivery, or field work.
✓ Strong answer
Gives a real, specific example
Sounds used to physical work, maybe even misses it
Describes managing energy, not just surviving the day
✗ Red flags
Their "hard day" was mostly sitting or standing still
Sounds like it exhausted or broke them
Can't think of a relevant example
Notes
Physically capable
III
Reliability & Trust
Can we trust them with money and a company vehicle?
Context for you
This person handles company money and a company vehicle — mostly unsupervised. You're not just checking their record. You're checking their relationship with accountability. Do they treat responsibility seriously or casually?
1 Trust & accountability must-have
"___, tell me about a time you were trusted with something that wasn't yours — money, equipment, someone else's property. What happened and how did you handle it?"
What you're listening for
Not whether they handled money before — but how they behaved when accountable for something valuable. Did they document? Over-communicate? The details in their story tell you everything.
✓ Strong answer
Specific story — real amounts, real context
Treated it with clear seriousness
Mentions communication, documentation, checking in
✗ Red flags
Can't think of an example — it was never required of them
Casual attitude — "yeah it was fine, no big deal"
Story has a gap or doesn't quite add up
If the story is vague — follow-up questions
"What would you have done if something went wrong with the amount?"
"Did you keep a record or paper trail of any kind?"
Notes
Trustworthy with responsibility
2 Driving must-have
"You'd be in a company vehicle every day, ___. Clean driving record — and I mean clean. Any tickets, accidents, anything in the last three years I should know about?"
What you're listening for
"And I mean clean" adds mild pressure. Someone with nothing to hide won't flinch. Someone with something to hide will hesitate — even slightly. Watch for that pause.
✓ Strong answer
Answers immediately — nothing to hide
If there was a minor incident, discloses it upfront and owns it
✗ Red flags
Hesitates before answering
Minimizes or downplays something that comes up
License issues — suspended, expired, restricted
Notes
Clean driving record
3 Saturday availability must-have
"Saturdays are required every single week, ___ — not every other, not most weeks. What does your Saturday currently look like, and what would you be giving up to do this?"
Why ask it this way
"What would you be giving up" invites honesty instead of the automatic yes. If they're genuinely fine with Saturdays, they'll say "not much." If there's a real conflict, this surfaces it before it becomes a no-show problem three weeks in.
✓ Strong answer
Says yes without any conditions attached
"Not much" or "nothing that can't shift" — clean and honest
✗ Hard disqualifier
"Usually" / "most weeks" / "as long as..." — that is a no
Secondary job, family conflict, or recurring commitment on Saturdays
Notes
Saturday confirmed — no conditions
IV
Mindset & Behavior
How do they actually operate when things get hard?
Context for you
Most candidates are coached on logistics questions. This section goes after who they actually are — how they handle pressure, frustration, and accountability when no one is watching. Listen for patterns, not polished answers.
1 Self-management under pressure jolt
"___, your day is planned. Two stops run long, one client won't answer the door, and it's already 3pm. You haven't eaten. Walk me through exactly what happens next."
What you're actually testing
Not just time management — emotional regulation. Does this person spiral when conditions aren't ideal, or do they stay functional? The details they add — or skip — reveal how self-aware they really are.
✓ Strong answer
Reprioritizes immediately — knows what can wait vs. what can't
Communicates proactively — calls ahead to next stop
Acknowledges frustration without being consumed by it
✗ Red flags
"I'd just push through" — no actual plan, just willpower talk
Would wait for someone to tell them what to do next
Hunger or frustration becomes the whole focus of the answer
If their answer is too clean — follow-up questions
"Be honest — what's the version where you're actually starting to get frustrated?"
"Have you had a day where everything stacked wrong? What did that actually look like?"
Notes
Self-manages under pressure
2 Accountability jolt
"Tell me about a time you made a mistake at work that affected someone else, ___. What did you actually do?"
What you're actually testing
The story matters less than how they tell it. Do they own it or subtly shift blame? Do they describe impact on the other person? Do they say what they changed — or just that they apologized? Real accountability sounds specific and a little uncomfortable.
✓ Strong answer
Owns it cleanly — no "but" or "technically it wasn't my fault"
Describes impact on others — not just how they felt
Says what they changed — not just that they apologized
✗ Red flags
The "mistake" ends up being someone else's fault by the end
Can't think of one — or picks something inconsequential
Rushes to "it worked out fine" without addressing the impact
Notes
Takes ownership without deflecting
3 Unsupervised behavior must-have jolt
"___, if I called your last manager right now and asked what you're like when no one's watching — what would they say?"
What you're actually testing
This role is unsupervised by design. You're hiring someone who does the right thing when it costs them nothing to cut corners. Their comfort level answering this tells you everything.
✓ Strong answer
Answers without pause — they know who they are
Includes a real self-awareness moment — something still in progress
Doesn't just pitch themselves — sounds like a real person
✗ Red flags
Long pause before a very polished answer — performing
Zero self-awareness — "they'd say I'm basically perfect"
Won't let you contact their last manager
If they say "they'd say I'm great" — follow-up questions
"What's one thing they'd want you to still work on?"
"Is there anything they wish you did differently?"
Notes
Trustworthy when unsupervised
V
Culture Fit
Would they thrive here? Would the team want them?
What this team actually looks like
High energy, move fastOwnership mentalityNo hand-holdingTeam-firstDirect communicationGritty & action-oriented
Context for you
Culture fit is about operating style. Someone who needs constant direction will slow the team down. These questions surface how someone actually functions — not how they want you to see them.
1 Environment fit culture
"___, what kind of work environment has made you feel the most like yourself — not just productive, actually like yourself?"
Why "like yourself" matters
It bypasses the rehearsed answer. You're asking where they came alive — not just where they performed. That filters out the candidate who's fine anywhere versus the one who genuinely fits here.
✓ Culture match
Talks about energy, movement, variety — not structure or predictability
Mentions momentum, team pace, figuring things out on the fly
Lights up describing it — you can feel it
✗ Culture mismatch
Describes quiet, routine, heavily structured environments fondly
Values close supervision or very detailed daily direction
Their best environment sounds nothing like this role
Notes
Environment aligns with ours
2 Ownership mentality culture
"___, tell me about a time the job asked more of you than was technically in your job description. What did you do?"
What you're listening for
You need someone who sees going beyond the list as normal, not as being taken advantage of. The answer reveals whether their default is ownership or compliance.
✓ Ownership
Stepped up without being asked — saw the gap and filled it
Talks about it like they'd do it again — not a grudge story
"I just figured it was the right call" energy
✗ Compliance
Resentful tone — felt used or taken advantage of
The story is actually about refusing or negotiating the ask
Can't think of a time — stays strictly in their lane
Notes
Ownership mentality
3 Team dynamic culture jolt
"What's the worst kind of teammate you've ever had to work with, ___? Not who — what made them hard to be around?"
What you're listening for
The teammate they can't stand reveals the teammate they are. If they hate people who don't carry their weight — they probably pull theirs. Vague answer like "just negativity" = low self-awareness.
✓ Good signal
Frustrated by laziness, blame-shifting, or dragging the team down
Specific and grounded — a real situation they've lived
Implies they hold themselves to a higher standard
✗ Watch for
Bothered by high performers or "brown-nosers" — hints at low drive
Vague — "just negativity I guess" — low self-awareness
Bad teammate was always "giving them feedback" — defensive about criticism
Notes
Team values align with ours

1 Compensation delivery
"Here's the structure, ___: you start at $700 a week. After 40 days — assuming things are going well — that moves to $800. The company vehicle and gas are fully covered, so zero transportation cost on your end. Any questions on that before we keep going?"
Delivery note
"Assuming things are going well" frames the increase as earned, not automatic. A confident candidate accepts that without flinching.
✓ Good reaction
Accepts it clearly — asks about the 40-day review or training
Recognizes the vehicle and gas as real value
No immediate pushback — trusts the process
✗ Watch for
Immediately tries to negotiate before even starting
Visibly deflated — was expecting something higher
Only asks when the first paycheck comes
Notes
Comp reaction is professional
2 Final — their turn jolt
"Last one, ___, and it's yours: what's the one thing about you that this interview probably didn't surface?"
Why end with this
One final chance to show you who they are on their own terms. Some reveal something genuinely great. Some go blank — which tells you they've been performing the whole time, and when the script runs out, there's nothing left.
✓ Strong close
Adds something real — a value, a belief about how they work
Confident, self-possessed — clearly knows who they are
Makes you want to hire them more than five minutes ago
✗ Weak close
"I think I covered everything..." — no self-knowledge
Restates something they already said
Goes back to salary or start date logistics
Notes
Strong close — knows who they are
