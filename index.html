II
Work History
3–9 min
Script
Observe · Listen For · Predict
Exit Speech
Let's start — Present reality
"Let's start with something simple — what are you currently doing for work right now?"
Follow up lightly
"How long have you been there?"
"What does a typical day look like for you?"
Step 3 — Bridge to satisfaction
"Got it. And how's that actually been for you so far?"
Open the door
"What do you enjoy about it?"
"What's been a little frustrating or missing?"
Step 4 — Contrast question
"If nothing changed there over the next 6 to 12 months — how would you feel about that?"
Step 5 — Earned why
"So with that in mind — what made you start exploring other opportunities right now?"
◎ Observe
Do they open up more as you go — or stay surface throughout?
Who is the villain in their story?
Tone shift on the contrast question — do they pause, or answer instantly?
The contrast question unlocks real urgency. Watch what it reveals.
✦ Strong signal
Owns their situation — "I stayed too long somewhere I outgrew"
Moving toward something — not just escaping
Contrast answer has real tension: "I'd feel stuck. I need to be moving."
⚑ Watch for
Every problem is someone else's fault
Contrast answer is flat: "I guess it'd be fine" — no urgency, no drive
"Just looking for something better" — no direction
◈ Predict
Strong
"Honestly? I'd be frustrated. I know I can do more than what I'm doing. I just haven't been in the right environment."
Average
"The pay isn't great and there's not much growth — I'd probably still be there but looking."
Watch
"I mean it'd be okay I guess. It's just a job." — no drive, no urgency
—
Notes
Real motivation — moving toward something with urgency
III
Prove Experience
9–13 min
Script
Observe · Listen For · Predict
Exit Speech
Ask — one real story must-prove
"Before I walk you through the role — give me one example of a time you had to manage multiple tasks or responsibilities at once without someone telling you exactly what to do. What happened?"
If they ramble
"Let me pause you — what was your specific role in that?"
Go deeper
"What did you prioritize first and why?"
"What would have happened if you hadn't stepped up?"
If too passive
"Tell me what you actually did — not what you would do."
◎ Observe
Do they have a story ready — or are they searching?
Pronouns — "I did" vs "we did"
Do they own their role — or credit circumstances?
✦ Strong
Specific — names the situation, what they did, what happened
Shows initiative — didn't wait to be told
Calm under pressure — not frantic energy
⚑ Weak
"I'd probably just..." — hypothetical, not real experience
Story ends with someone else solving it
Can't think of a real example
◈ Predict
Strong
"At my last job, our coordinator was out and I had 4 pickups and a bank run on the same day. I mapped it out myself, did the urgent ones first, texted updates to the team, and got everything done."
Average
"I've had busy days where I had to figure things out — I usually just make a list and work through it."
Watch
"I mean I'm pretty organized, I'd just handle it as it comes" — no story, no specifics
Exit — can't prove initiative
"___, I appreciate you sharing that. I want to be straight with you — this role requires someone who can figure things out in the field without being told every step. What I'm hearing is more about what you'd do rather than what you've actually done. That gap matters here, and I don't want to set you up for something that's not the right fit."
Use after the jolt and follow-ups — not after the first soft answer.
Notes
Proved it — specific, shows initiative, owns their role
IV
Company Overview
13–17 min
Script
Observe · Listen For · Predict
Exit Speech
Say — Transition
"Awesome — let me give you a quick picture of what we do and where this role fits in."
Company overview — read aloud
"We're a roofing and general contracting company based in the US. Most of our work comes through insurance claims — storm damage, hail, that kind of thing. We have a full team in the field every day running appointments, doing inspections, capturing drone footage, and keeping jobs moving. The Field Runner is essentially the engine that keeps that operation running. You'd be supporting the production and sales teams with things like picking up payments from clients, making bank deposits, capturing drone photos at job sites, and helping with roofing inspections. It's a busy, mobile role — no two days look the same. Here's what I want you to know: the people who've done well in this role didn't stay here long. Not because they left — but because they moved up. If you're someone who shows initiative, picks things up fast, and puts their hand up for new responsibilities — this role becomes a launchpad, not just a job. We've seen Runners move into field roles, inspection roles, and sales within a year. The vehicle and gas are covered. Training is paid from day one. We just need someone reliable, organized, and willing to work."
Check
"Does that make sense so far? Does it still sound like something you'd want to explore?"
◎ Observe
Do they connect it to their own experience while you explain?
Do they ask a question — or just stay quiet?
Does the "launchpad" framing excite them — or do they not react?
✦ Strong signal
Lights up at the growth angle: "Oh so there's room to move up?"
Asks a smart question — territory, what a typical day looks like
Connects it to something they've done or want
⚑ Watch for
No reaction to the growth angle — just wants the job, any job
First question is only about pay — no curiosity about the role
Energy drops after the overview
◈ Predict
Strong
"Yeah that actually sounds like exactly what I was looking for — I want something where I can prove myself and grow from there"
Average
"Okay, sounds good. So I'd be doing different things every day?"
Watch
"Yeah sure, sounds fine. What does it pay?" — no engagement with the role itself
—
Notes
Engaged with the role — growth angle resonated
V
Physical & Safety
17–20 min
Script
Observe · Listen For · Predict
Exit Speech
Heights must-have
"Part of this role involves getting on rooftops — including steep ones — for inspections and drone photos. I want to be upfront about that. Walk me through your relationship with heights — be real with me."
If they hesitate
"What's the highest you've been on a ladder or roof before?"
"If you got up there and it felt worse than you expected — what would you do?"
Physical stamina must-have
"Describe the most physically demanding workday you've had. What was it, and how did you feel by the end of it?"
◎ Observe
Body language shift on heights — arms cross, voice drops?
Do they answer quickly and confidently — or hedge?
Stamina story — is their "hard day" actually physical?
✦ Strong
Heights: has real experience — construction, military, cleaning, roofing
Asks about safety protocols — engaged, not reckless
Stamina: gives a specific, physical example — sounds used to it
⚑ Hard disqualifier
"I think I can handle it" — still uncertain after you've made it safe to say no
Asks if the roof part can be avoided
Stamina "hard day" was mostly sitting or standing
◈ Predict
Strong
"Yeah, no problem — I've been on plenty of roofs doing construction. Heights don't bother me."
Watch
"I mean I think I'd be okay... I haven't really been on a roof before but I can try"
Exit — genuine fear of heights
"___, I really appreciate your honesty — and I mean that. This part of the role is something we can't work around, and I'd rather be straight with you than put you in a situation that's not the right fit. You've got good qualities and I think there's a role out there that's a better match. I'll keep your info on file and if something comes up that doesn't involve rooftop work, we'll reach out."
Use only after you've made it safe to say no. A candidate who's still hesitant after that is a liability and a retention risk.
Notes
Comfortable with heights — physically capable
VI
Reliability & Trust
20–23 min
Script
Observe · Listen For · Predict
Exit Speech
Trust & accountability must-have
"Tell me about a time you were trusted with something that wasn't yours — money, equipment, someone else's property. What happened and how did you handle it?"
If vague
"What would you have done if something went wrong with the amount?"
"Did you keep a record or paper trail?"
Driving must-have
"You'd be in a company vehicle every day. Clean driving record — and I mean clean. Any tickets, accidents, anything in the last three years I should know about?"
Saturday must-have
"Saturdays are required every week — not most weeks, every week. What does your Saturday currently look like and what would you be giving up?"
◎ Observe
Trust story — how seriously do they take responsibility? Casual or earnest?
Driving — any pause before answering? That pause is data.
Saturday — even a half-second hesitation matters. Watch their face.
✦ Strong
Trust: specific story, real amounts, mentions documentation or communication
Driving: answers immediately, nothing to hide
Saturday: "Not much" — clean, no conditions attached
⚑ Hard disqualifiers
Trust: "No big deal" casual attitude about handling money
Driving: hesitates, then minimizes something that comes up
Saturday: "Usually" / "most weeks" / "as long as..." — that is a no
◈ Predict
Strong
"Yeah, I was the keyholder at my last job — opened and closed the register daily. I always counted twice and documented everything."
Watch
"Yeah I mean I've handled cash before, no big deal" — no seriousness about it
Exit — fails a must-have
"___, I want to be upfront with you. [Heights / Saturday availability / driving record] is something we can't work around in this role. I'd rather tell you now than waste your time. It doesn't reflect on you as a person — it's just a hard requirement for this specific position."
Customize the bracketed part for whichever must-have is the issue.
Notes
Trustworthy · clean record · Saturday confirmed

    Script

    Exit Speech
    Say — Pay
    "Here's the structure: you start at $700 a week. After 40 days — assuming things are going well — that moves to $800. The company vehicle and gas are fully covered, so zero transportation cost on your end."
    Ask — reaction
    "Any questions on the pay before we keep going?"
    Ask — income check
    "What are you making right now and what are you looking to get to?"
    If their goal is significantly above $800/week — be honest about the ceiling and see if they're still interested in the growth path.
    ◎ Observe
    Face and energy when you say the numbers — relieved, disappointed, neutral?
    First question after the pay reveal — what do they care about most?
    Do they see the vehicle/gas as real value — or ignore it?
    ✦ Strong reaction
    Accepts it clearly — asks about the 40-day review or what "going well" means
    Recognizes vehicle + gas as real additional value
    Interested in the growth path more than just the starting number
    ⚑ Watch for
    Immediately tries to negotiate before starting
    Visibly deflated — was expecting significantly more
    Only asks when the first paycheck comes
    ◈ Predict

    "That works for me. What does the 40-day review look like — is it automatic or based on performance?"
    Average
    "Okay, that's fine. When would I start?"
